Kinematics

A particle is given an impulse giving an inital velocity of \(x_0\). It then has an acceleration given by \(a=-bv\) where \(b\) is a constant.
Obtain an expression for \(v(t)\).

So, let's begin by writing the equation for acceleration as \[\frac{dv}{dt}=-bv\] If we divide by \(v\) and multiply by \(dt\) we get \[\frac{dv}{v}=-bdt\] Now, if we intergrate both sides we get \[\int {\frac{dv}{v}}=\int {-b\,dt}\] \[ln\,v=-bt+c\] And then raising everything to the power of \(e\) \[v=e^{-bt}e^c\] Let \(e^c=v_0\) where \(v_0\) is the inital velocity. \[v=v_0e^{-bt}\] Boom! Solved! This is known as separation of variables. Kinda important.

It's important to note a key relationship between the potential of a particle and the force it experiences. \[F=-\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}\] Beautiful.

Fermat's Principle

Fermat's priciple states that

"Nature operates by means and ways that are easiest and fastest."

This simple idea will allow us to derive equations of motion for any system (including quantum!).

Let's look at a good example of this - Snell's Law.

The Lagrangian

\[L(x,v)=T(v)-V(x)\] Where \(T(v)\) is the kinetic energy and \(v(x)\) is the potential energy.
Therefore, for a one-dimensional partical when \(V(x)=mgx\) is \[L(x,v)=\frac{1}{2}mv^2-mgx\]

Action

Now that I've told you a new idea, how about another one?
Mathematically definded as: \[\mathcal{A}=\int_{t_0}^{t_1} L\,dt\]

The Euler-Lagrange Equation

Let's derive the priciple of least action.
Let's start from the equation for action. \[\mathcal{A}=\int_{t_0}^{t_1} L\,dt\] Next, we need to use the calculus of variations - by adding a very small value to the position and velocity. \[Action=\int_{t_0}^{t_1} ùêø(x+h,v+\dot{h})\] Then using the Taylor Expansion we can find that: \[Action=\int_{t_0}^{t_1} ùêø(x,v)+\frac{h}{1!}\frac{\partial L}{\partial x}+\frac{\dot{h}}{1!}\frac{\partial L}{\partial v}+ ...\] We can keep on going for higher powers but this isn't important.

This is because when \(h\lt\lt1\) we find that \(h^1\lt\lt h^2\) and \(h^1\) is bigger than all higher powers of \(h\)

Therfore, the first order terms of the expansion are equal to zero. \[0=\int_{t_0}^{t_1} h\frac{\partial L}{\partial x}+\dot{h}\frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\] \[0=\int_{t_0}^{t_1} h\frac{\partial L}{\partial x}+\frac{dh}{ht}\frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\] Now we can use intergration by parts to simplify the second part of the above equation. \[\int_{t_0}^{t_1} \frac{dh}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial v}=\biggl[h\frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\biggl]_{t_0}^{t_1}-\int_{t_0}^{t_1} h\frac{d}{dt}\biggl(\frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\biggr)\] For the path of least action ... Therefore we can say that \[\biggl[h\frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\biggl]_{t_0}^{t_1} = 0\] This means our intergration by parts is equal to \[\int_{t_0}^{t_1} \frac{dh}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial v}=-\int_{t_0}^{t_1} h\frac{d}{dt}\biggl(\frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\biggr)\] Subbing this we find that \[0=\int_{t_0}^{t_1} h\frac{\partial L}{\partial x}-\int_{t_0}^{t_1} h\frac{d}{dt}\biggl(\frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\biggr)\] If we factorise out the h \[0=\int_{t_0}^{t_1} h\biggl(\frac{\partial L}{\partial x}-\frac{d}{dt}\biggl(\frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\biggr)\biggr)\] It's important to note that h can be any value. Therefore for the above to be true we have to assume that \[\frac{d}{dt}\biggl(\frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\biggr)-\frac{\partial L}{\partial x}=0\] And this is the Euler-Lagrange equation. Woohoo!

We'll begin by asuming we'll be using polar cordinates for this example (the euler-lagrange equation works
in both cartesian and polar coordinates, see later). Let's also note that \(v(x)=mgh\) and \(T(v)=\frac{1}{2}mv^{2}\)
Therefore we need to trasform the height and velocity into polar cordinates. \[h=l-lcos\theta\] \[v=l\omega\] As \(l\) is constant this works. Note that for if we let \(v(x)=mgh\) Then the lagrangian equals: \[L=\frac{1}{2}mv^{2}-mgh\] If we sub in our new equations for h and x into our lagrangian we get: \[L=\frac{1}{2}m\omega^{2}l^{2}-mgl+mglcos\theta\] Then using the euler-lagrange equation we find that \[m\dot{\omega}l^{2}+mglsin\theta=0\] Dividing by m and l \[\dot{\omega}l+gsin\theta=0\] Cheeky rearrange \[\dot{\omega}=\frac{gsin\theta}{l}\] And if we approached this from a newtonian perspective we would get the same answer.

Note that both \(m_1\) and \(m_2\) have the same velocity, this means the kinetic energy is \[T=\frac{1}{2}m_1v^2+\frac{1}{2}m_2v^2\] and the potential energy is \[V=m_1gx_1+m_2gx_2\] However, as \(x_1+m_2=l\) we get \[V=m_1gx_1+m_2gl-m_2gx_1\] Then our lagrange is \[L=\frac{1}{2}m_1v^2+\frac{1}{2}m_2v^2-m_1gx_1-m_2gl+m_2gx_1\] Now finding the euler-lagrange equation is \[\frac{d}{dt}\biggl(\frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\biggr)=m_1a+m_2a\] \[\frac{\partial L}{\partial x}=-m_1g+m_2g\] \[m_1a+m_2a=-m_1g+m_2g\] Cheeky rearrange. \[a(m_1+m_2)=g(m_2-m_1)\] \[a=\frac{m_2-m_1}{m_2+m_1}g\]

is merely a moral, and not a physical one. It is not, and cannot be, the cause of any effect in Nature."

However, this idea changed in 1948 due to Richard Feynman.

The idea was that a particle can take every path through configuration space, but the probability amplitude of taking that path varies according to \[\psi=e^{i\frac{\mathcal{A}}{\hbar}}\] Where \(\mathcal{A}\) is the action of that specific path.

Therfore, to calculate the probability amplitude of going from point A to point B we sum all of the probabilities. \[\psi_{A\to B}=\sum_{i=1}^{N}e^{i\frac{\mathcal{A}}{\hbar}}\] However there are normally infinatly many ways a particle can get from A to B.
Therefore we have to replace our sum with an intergal. \[\psi_{A\to B}=\int_{A\to B}Dq(t)e^{i\frac{\mathcal{A}}{\hbar}}\] The expression on the right side is known as the path intergral Now to calculate the total probabilty of getting from A to B, sqaure this value. \[P(A\to B)=|\psi_{A\to B}|^2\] Now, this still doesn't make much sense, let's look at euler identity.



We can see that \(\psi_{A\to B}\) lies on a unit circle.

However different paths will have different actions meaning the 'vector' for \(\psi_{A\to b}\) will point in different directions.

Therefore when we sum these 'vectors' we could end up with a very small vector (low probabilty) or a
very large arrow (high probabilty).

When we get a small arrow we have had destructive interference as most of the arrows
have cancelled out. But, when we have a large arrow we have had constructive interference
as most of the arrows have added together.

The Classical Limit

In the quantum realm, the action for a path is rather small. However in the classical realm the
action is rather big. This is because the action \(L=\frac{1}{2}mv^2-V(x)\) depends on mass.

Therefor, when we have a large mass, the difference is \(\psi\) between close paths will be very large
and so we will get destructive interference. However, in the quantum realm the difference in action
between two close paths is quite similar to we get constructive interference.

This is why in the classical world the particles only takes the paths near the true path but in
the quantum realm the particle can take mutiple paths (think double slit experiment).
