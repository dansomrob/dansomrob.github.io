State Augmentation

Let's look at the eqution for a spring with fricion. \[\ddot x(t) = -kx(t) - b\dot x(t)\] This is a second order differential equation.
However if we define the state \(z(t)\) as \[z(t)=\begin{bmatrix}\dot x(t) \\ x(t)\end{bmatrix}\] Then we can rewrite this equation as \[\begin{bmatrix}\ddot x(t) \\ \dot x(t)\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}-b & -k\\1 & 0\end{bmatrix}\cdot\begin{bmatrix}\dot x(t) \\ x(t)\end{bmatrix}\] If \(A\) is defined as \[A=\begin{bmatrix}-b & -k\\1 & 0\end{bmatrix}\] We get \[\dot z(t)=A\cdot z(t)\] Which is a first order equation!
This is called state augmentation and allows us to simplfy some harder differential equations.

Looking at the second order equation \[\ddot y = By\] we can define \(z(t)\) as \[z(t)=\begin{bmatrix}\dot y(t)\\y(t)\end{bmatrix}\] Which gives us \[\dot z(t) = \begin{bmatrix}0 & B\\I & 0\end{bmatrix}\cdot z(t)\] A recast of a higher oder equation as a first order equation.

Eigendecomposition

Many people learn the mechanics of finding eigenvalues and eigenvectors but not much more than that.
Eigendecomposition takes a system and finds the modes of the system. Modes are unchanging
parts of the system - eg saddle points.

If we look at the eigenequation \[Av=\lambda v\] where \(A\) is a matrix, \(\lambda\) is the eigenvalue and \(v\) is the eigenvector.

If we apply a transformation \(A\) to a vector \(v\) and the direction of the vector
is the same before and after the transformation, we call it an eigenvector.
This eigenvector thougth may be scaled by a scalar (eg. twice as long - \(\lambda=2\))
we is called the eigenvalue.

It's important to note that for all diagonizable linear systems we only have two behavours.
\(\Rightarrow\)For Real\(\lambda\) < 0 we have stable dynamics.
\(\Rightarrow\)For Real\(\lambda\) > 0 we have unstable dynamics.
